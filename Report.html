Hypothesis & Data Source

Data Source & Selection

Five seasons of data were collected between 2014 to 2018. Post 2018 seasons do not have enough games to support meaningful analysis due to COVID-19. 635 NBA players were further selected based on the criteria of having played more than 200 minutes in a single season.

The following websites provide most official NBA player data on height, wingspan, position, hometown, Player Efficiency Rating (PER), Win Shares (WS), Salary, and Team.

  • https://www.basketball-reference.com/
  • https://hoopshype.com/

However, as ethnicity was not provided by these two sites, Wikipedia was used to supplement this information.

Here, we categorize players into four classes: black, white, mixed and other.

All raw data used in charts and graphs within this report can be accessed via the github link https://github.com/hankyang0306 .

Term Definitions

  • Player Efficiency Rating (PER): Holistic measurement of a player’s performance; it boils down all of a player’s contributions into one number through a specific formula.

  • Win Share (WS): A statistic that assigns credit for a team’s success to individuals on the team.

  • Black Players: Includes both African American players and players from foreign countries.

  • White Players: Includes both White American players and players from foreign countries.

    Hypothesis and Public Opinion

    • Are Black NBA players physically advantaged over White NBA players?
    • Is there a pay discrepancy between Black and White NBA players?

Section - II : Salary Comparison by Race

To make the Player Efficiency Rating (PER) vs Salary trend more apparent, the following regression lines are drawn.

In general, White players are paid more despite having the same PER when compared with Black players. For example, Note at 20 PER, Black players are projected to earn 25M, while white players earn 35M.

A Similar trend can be observed when comparing Win Shares (WS) vs Salary.

This pay discrepancies and the reasons behind this difference become more evident when we break down this comparison by positions. We will compare Shooting Guards [SG], Power Forward [PF], and Centers [C]. Point Guards and Small Forwards were not considered because there were insufficient data points for White players for any meaningful analysis.

The above charts indicate that for SG and PFs, White players are projected to earn more salary for the same PER as Black athletes. However, this projection is reversed for the Center position, as Black players in fact have a higher salary for the same PER. Incidentally, Center is the position with the most White players, so this observation could be attributed to an increase in competition in the Center position.

 

Outlier Analysis

We need to analyze outliers to determine if they are indeed valid data points that represent the general trend identified previously. Outliers need to be removed to present the most accurate projection of the data.

Looking at White players, Chandler Parsons and Nicolas Batum both had unusual contracts because of injuries and other external factors, which affected their performances and limited their number of games. These data points, thus, should be removed since it does not represent the general trend of Salary vs PER.

As for Black players, outliers can be identified as well. Allen Crabbe averaged only 9.6 points per game in 2018-2019 and Solomon Hill only averaged 4.3 points per game in 2018-2019. Their performances do not logically correspond to their salary, and thus do not represent the general trend as well, establishing both data points as outliers. However, Andrew Wiggins, although his data point is slightly outstanding, averaged 18.1 points per game in 2018-2019. He is not an invalid data point and must be included in the data set.

With these invalid data points removed, the linear regression lines are much closer to each other.

Salary vs Win Share analysis also produces nearly identical regression lines between Black and White NBA players.Initially, PER vs. Salary analysis indicates a 10M average pay difference between AA & WA. In reality, after removing outliers, the projections suggest that Black and White players are paid similarly.

Section - III：Case Studies by Team

We chose the Utah Jazz (8th seed), Charlotte Hornets (17th) and New York Knicks (30th) from the 2018-2018 season. These teams were selected because they have enough spread in team rank and have a sufficient number of white players for meaningful analysis.

By comparing the three teams above, we can observe a direct correlation between Salary vs PER. There is a strong negative relationship for the New York Knicks, which may suggest flaws on the management level since awarding players with poor performances with a higher salary is not logical. There is a slight negative relationship for Charlotte Hornets, and there is a strong positive relationship for the Utah Jazz.

A similar trend can be observed from Salary vs Win Shares. This difference in regression lines for these three teams also suggests the reasons behind their respective ranks in the league.

We study each team’s situation with Salary vs PER and Salary vs WS between Black and White players.

New York Knicks

The regression lines for both graphs suggest illogical decision making on the leadership level of the organization, as there is still a strong negative relationship for both Salary vs PER and Salary vs Win Shares for Black and White players. We will not consider the Knicks.

Charlotte Hornets

Utah Jazz

From the perspective of the Charlotte Hornets, there is a strong positive relationship for Salary vs Win Shares for both races, but in terms of Salary vs PER, White players display a negative relationship, while Black players display a positive one.

On the other hand, the Utah Jazz display positive relationships for Black and White players for both Salary vs PER, and Salary vs WS.

Why is there an abnormal negative relationship for Salary vs PER in terms of White players of the Charlotte Hornets?

We dive deeper into the specific White players of the Hornets and compare them with the Jazz.

It is observed that the Hornets have three White players playing the same position at Center, while the Jazz have a more diverse rotation, where none of them play Center in fact. Recalling the earlier analysis where Salary vs PER was broken down by positions, the Hornet’s increased competition at the Center position may have contributed to the negative relationship seen in Salary vs PER. However, there also seems to be a link to poor franchise management, since the evident difference in PER between White and Black players suggests illogical decision making. Perhaps each of these Centers showed hints of promise, causing the Hornets to identify them as particularly outstanding among their peers, and award them with large contracts. Unfortunately, none of these centers have had fantastic careers, thus invalidating their contracts.

 

 

Conclusion and Future Work

 

Tackling hypothesis 1:

  • Wingspan / Height analysis seems to suggest that Black players are indeed physically advantaged. However, this advantage fades as player height increases, and White Players become more dominant.

     

Tackling hypothesis 2:

  • Initially, PER vs. Salary analysis indicates an average pay difference between Black and White players, where White players earn more at the same PER. However, removing outliers reveals that Black players and White players are paid similarly.
  • By breaking down by positions and through case studies, comparisons within Point guards and Power Forwards show that Black have to perform better in order to earn the same salary as WA, but this trend is reversed for the Center position (More White players), indicating that it is increased competition that affects salary.

 

Acknowledgement of insufficiencies and future work:

  • The study classifies Black and White players of the NBA as those from both the US and foreign countries. A future study will aim to break down the analysis of Salary vs PER, Salary vs WS between players from the US, and those born beyond the states. Will the projections in these cases be the same as those we produced in this study?
  • This study also omits Asian and Latino players. This is because these players fall in a mere 0.6% of all NBA players. A future study will aim to determine the reasons behind the lack of representation of these other races in the NBA.
  • An analysis on the hometowns of players was done as well, but was not included due to its insignificance to the subject hypothesis.
